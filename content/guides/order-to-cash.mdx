# Order to Cash (O2C)

According to **APQC** standards, the **Order to Cash (O2C)** process encompasses the entire lifecycle of receiving an order from a customer and successfully capturing payment for that order. In E-Commerce, the speed and accuracy of the O2C cycle is the primary driver of customer satisfaction and corporate revenue flow.

## Node Knowledge: The O2C Lifecycle
1. **Order Capture:** A customer places an order on the digital storefront.
2. **Order Management:** The system validates inventory, fraud risk, and routes the order to the correct fulfillment center.
3. **Fulfillment:** Picking, packing, and shipping the physical units.
4. **Payment Capture:** Authorizing and eventually settling the credit card or digital wallet transaction.

## Key BA Activities (BABOK® Alignment)

In the *Business Analysis Body of Knowledge (BABOK®)*, analyzing O2C relies heavily on the **Solution Evaluation** and **Requirements Analysis** knowledge areas, particularly focusing on mitigating bottlenecks.

*   **Data Modeling:** Documenting the transition of states for the central "Order" entity as it touches the Website, the Order Management System (OMS), and the Warehouse Management System (WMS).
*   **Root Cause Analysis:** Investigating why orders get "stuck" in the system (e.g., inventory allocation failures).
*   **Non-Functional Requirements Validation:** Ensuring the OMS can handle peak order volumes during flash sales (e.g., Black Friday).

## Typical Deliverables

*   **Context Diagram:** A high-level visual showing the flow of data between the E-Commerce front-end (e.g., Shopify), the OMS, the Payment Gateway (e.g., Stripe), and the ERP.
*   **Functional Specification Document (FSD):** Detailing the exact API payloads generated when a customer clicks "Checkout".

## 💡 Expert BA Review & Adjustments

> [!TIP]
> **From the Desk of a Lead Business Analyst**
> The most critical point of failure in modern e-commerce O2C is "Inventory Allocation". 
> Do not just document *how* an order is created. Document *when* the inventory is secured. If a customer adds an item to their cart, is it held? Or is it only deducted upon successful credit card capture? Getting this wrong leads to overselling, which destroys brand trust.
